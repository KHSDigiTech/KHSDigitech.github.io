The Game Design Document (GDD) is your game's blueprint. It serves as both a planning tool and assessment evidence. Complete each section thoughtfully - this document will guide your entire development process and demonstrate your design thinking to assessors.

Assessment Integration

This document directly supports both assessment standards:

AS91893: Evidence of planning and design process
AS91897: Technical specifications and implementation planning

üéÆ Game Concept

Game Title

Write your game's title here. Choose something memorable and relevant to your game's theme.

One-Line Description

Describe your game in one sentence. This should capture the core gameplay and appeal.

Example:

"A fast-paced 2D platformer where players control a ninja cat collecting fish while avoiding robot dogs in a cyberpunk city."

Genre & Platform

Specify your game's genre (e.g., platformer, puzzle, shooter) and target platform (PC, mobile, web).

Target Audience

Who is this game for? Consider age range, gaming experience, and interests.

üéØ Core Gameplay

Primary Objective

What is the main goal players are trying to achieve? Be specific about win/lose conditions.

Core Mechanics

List the 3-5 most important gameplay mechanics. These are the actions players will perform most often.

Example Core Mechanics:

  • Jump and double-jump to navigate platforms
  • Dash through enemies and obstacles
  • Collect fish to increase score
  • Avoid or defeat robot enemies

Controls

Map out your control scheme. Keep it simple - players should be able to learn controls quickly.

Visual Style

Color Palette

What colors will dominate your game? Consider mood and readability.

Audio Style

Describe the music and sound effects style that will complement your visuals.

UI Design

How will menus, HUD, and interface elements look and feel?

üìä Project Scope

Must-Have Features

List the absolutely essential features your game needs to be playable and meet assessment requirements.

Should-Have Features

Features that would improve the game but aren't critical for core functionality.

Could-Have Features

Nice-to-have features you might add if time permits (stretch goals).

Scope Management Tip:

Focus on making your "Must-Have" features excellent rather than trying to implement everything. A polished simple game beats an unfinished complex one!
